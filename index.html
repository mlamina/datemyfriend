Each friend, in their own words.

Every friend you give a QR to writes their own intro — a sentence or two about you, in their own voice. Julie's reads like Julie. Your college roommate's reads like her.

They show the QR when the moment comes.

At a party, on a hike, at a dinner — anywhere a real introduction would happen. Whoever they hand it to scans on their own phone.

The person leaves you a note, if they're interested.

Their name, a photo, a sentence about themselves. You decide whether to reach out — by text, like a human.

The mechanics

Why this works.

  • —Your friends are the filter. They only show your QR to people they'd actually want to introduce you to.
  • —You don't have to swipe. You don't have to open the app. Your friends are out in the world meeting people for you.
  • —Your friends actually want you happy. They have skin in the game — the algorithm doesn't.
  • —Introductions happen in real life. A party, a dinner, a hike — the way it worked before apps and feeds and looksmaxxing.
  • —No stranger ever sees your profile. No public link, no discovery feed, no screenshots leaked into a group chat.
  • —You decide who gets your number. Notes come in. You text whoever you want. Nobody else gets through.
A small manifesto
“

Dating apps make money when you keep swiping. We make money when you stop.

The matchmaker has been a human role for ten thousand years. Outsourcing it to an algorithm was a thirteen-year experiment, and most of us are tired.

Your friends know you. They know what you find funny, what you can't tolerate, who broke your heart and how. Trust them with this.

The whole product is a profile, a friend, and a sentence they wrote about you. We just made it possible.

A note from the founder
Photo placeholder — Marco

I'm Marco. I built this because every person I love in San Francisco has, at some point in the last three years, told me the same thing: they're done with the apps, and they wish their friends would just introduce them to people again.

The problem isn't that friends don't want to. It's that everyone moved. Everyone got busy. Everyone forgot it was part of the job. I wanted to make it small and ritual again — a card a friend hands you, a sentence they wrote, a person on the other end of it.

If you sign up, you'll hear from me directly. There is no team to forward you to.

Marco
